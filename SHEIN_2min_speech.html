Slide 1–2：环境与生命周期（约 2 分钟）| Slide 3：治理、TPB 与建议（补充，约 60–90 秒）| 风格：雅思 6.5，简单清晰

主稿（Slide 1–3，按 PPT 页码）

[Slide 1 - 开场：问题与框架] Good morning everyone. Our question is simple: can ultra-fast fashion really be sustainable? My answer uses one clear logic: we must evaluate SHEIN across the whole life cycle, not only one step. So I will follow four links: raw materials, production, logistics, and end-of-life. My core claim is: reducing inventory waste is progress, but it is not equal to reducing total environmental impact. [Slide 2 - 核心链条分析] First, raw materials. SHEIN still relies heavily on polyester, around 82%. Polyester is fossil-fuel based and hard to degrade, so the system starts with a high-carbon foundation. Second, production. SHEIN's on-demand small-batch model can reduce unsold stock, and this is a real efficiency gain. But sustainability is about total impact. In 2024, Scope 3 emissions still increased by 23.1%. So the growth of total volume is faster than efficiency improvement. Third, logistics. SHEIN ships many small parcels across countries. This gives consumers speed and convenience, but it also creates high transport emissions. So in this model, fast delivery and low carbon are still in tension. Fourth, end-of-life. Many items are low-price and short-use, so disposal happens quickly. Synthetic fabrics also release microplastics during washing and after disposal. This means environmental pressure does not stop at checkout; it continues after use. [Slide 3 - 治理、消费者行为（TPB）与三层建议] Now we move beyond the physical life cycle, to governance and consumer behaviour. First, governance. A simple rule on this slide is: policy on paper is not the same as effective enforcement. In the EU, authorities have raised concerns about misleading sustainability claims and deceptive labels. Consumer groups such as BEUC also criticise dark patterns — for example fake countdown timers, low-stock pressure, and confirm-shaming. These tools push people to buy more, not to buy less. Second, we can read this through the Theory of Planned Behaviour. Attitude: the products feel cheap and easy to justify. Subjective norms: trends and FOMO create social pressure to keep up. Perceived behavioural control: the app makes ordering very fast and frictionless. Together, these drivers reinforce overconsumption, and many of them are reinforced by design. Third, our recommendations sit on three levels. For enterprises: third-party verified reporting, design for durability, and real circularity — not only green branding. For regulators: extended producer responsibility, stronger action against greenwashing, and clearer rules on dark patterns. For consumers: more transparent information, a shift from volume to value, and fair pricing so better choices are realistic — not only a moral duty on individuals. Finally, three closing points on the slide. One: fast fashion is a full life-cycle problem, from raw materials to disposal. Two: SHEIN optimises the wrong metric — efficiency of waste per item, not reduction of waste and scale at the system level. Three: genuine sustainability needs coordinated change across firms, regulators, and consumption patterns — not a single fix. Thank you.
