主稿（Slide 1–3，按 PPT 页码）

[Slide 1 - 开场：问题与框架] Good morning everyone. Our question is simple: can ultra-fast fashion really be sustainable? My answer uses one clear logic: we must evaluate SHEIN across the whole life cycle, not only one step. So I will follow four links: raw materials, production, logistics, and end-of-life. My core claim is: reducing inventory waste is progress, but it is not equal to reducing total environmental impact. [Slide 2 - 核心链条分析] First, raw materials. SHEIN still relies heavily on polyester, around 82%. Polyester is fossil-fuel based and hard to degrade, so the system starts with a high-carbon foundation. Second, production. SHEIN's on-demand small-batch model can reduce unsold stock, and this is a real efficiency gain. But sustainability is about total impact. In 2024, Scope 3 emissions still increased by 23.1%. So the growth of total volume is faster than efficiency improvement. Third, logistics. SHEIN ships many small parcels across countries. This gives consumers speed and convenience, but it also creates high transport emissions. So in this model, fast delivery and low carbon are still in tension. Fourth, end-of-life. Many items are low-price and short-use, so disposal happens quickly. Synthetic fabrics also release microplastics during washing and after disposal. This means environmental pressure does not stop at checkout; it continues after use. [Slide 3 - 治理、消费者与收尾（口语流，勿逐条念 PPT）] So that was the physical chain. [pause] This slide is about governance and demand — who shapes the rules, and why people still buy so fast. Rules on paper are not the same as what you see in practice. [pause] The EU pushes back on misleading green claims, and groups like BEUC criticise dark patterns — fake timers, low-stock pressure — that nudge people toward more buying, not less. On demand we use Theory of Planned Behaviour, but in plain English: cheap prices, FOMO, and a frictionless checkout stack together into overconsumption, and much of that is by design. So the fix is shared — not “shoppers, try harder.” Firms need real accountability; regulators need teeth on EPR, greenwashing, and dark patterns; users need fair information and fair prices. To close: fast fashion stays a life-cycle problem. SHEIN cuts waste per item well, but that is not the same as cutting total waste when the system keeps scaling — so firms, rules, and everyday consumption have to move together. Thank you.
